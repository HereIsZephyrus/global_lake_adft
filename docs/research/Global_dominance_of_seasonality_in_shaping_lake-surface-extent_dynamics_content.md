## Abstract

Lakes are crucial for ecosystems1, greenhouse gas emissions2 and water resources3, yet their surface-extent dynamics, particularly seasonality, remain poorly understood at continental to global scales owing to limitations in satellite observations4,5. Although previous studies have focused on long-term changes6–8, comprehensive assessments of seasonality have been constrained by trade-offs between spatial resolution and temporal resolution in single-source satellite data. Here we show that seasonality is the dominant driver of lake-surface-extent variations globally. By leveraging a deep-learning-based spatiotemporal fusion of MODIS and Landsat-based datasets, combined with high-performance computing, we achieved monthly mapping of 1.4 million lakes (2001–2023). Our approach yielded basin-level median user’s and producer’s accuracies of 93% and 96%, respectively, when validated against the Global Surface Water dataset7. Seasonality-dominated lakes constitute 66% of the global lake area and approximately 60% of total lake counts, with over 90% of the world’s population residing in regions where such lakes prevail. During seasonality-induced extreme events, the impacts can exceed the combined magnitude of 23-year long-term changes and regular seasonal variations, doubling the contraction of 42% of shrinking lakes and fully offsetting the expansion of 45% of growing lakes. These results uncover previously hidden seasonal dynamics that are crucial for understanding hydrospheric responses to environmental changes9, protecting lacustrine systems10–12 and improving global climate models13,14. Our findings underscore the importance of incorporating seasonality into future research and suggest that advancements in the fusion of multisource remote-sensing data offer a promising path forward.

Lakes cover $3 . 7 \%$ of Earth’s non-glaciated land surface and contain $0 . 8 \%$ of the planet’s non-frozen terrestrial water storage15–17. Despite their relatively small footprint, lakes have a crucial role in maintaining ecosystem health1 , regulating greenhouse gas emissions2 , and serving as indicators of hydrological responses to climate change and human activities9 . Lakes support over 100,000 species that contribute to global biodiversity1 and substantially influence the global carbon cycle13. Furthermore, they are fundamental components of the hydrological cycle and are essential for water-resource management3 . A lake’s surface extent is one of its key characteristics18, critically determining the horizontal reach of littoral habitats10, the fractions of inundated and exposed lake beds19, and its overall water-storage capacity20. Changes in lake surface extent are direct reflections of their responses to climate change and human interventions affecting their mass balance6 . Understanding the distribution and spatiotemporal dynamics of lake surface extent is of critical scientific and practical importance.

Lake surface extent fluctuates across various temporal scales21, including long-term decadal trends of expansion or shrinkage, interannual variations driven by wet–dry cycles, and intra-annual changes between desiccation and inundation. The interaction of these factors intricately determines a lake’s surface extent at any given time, reflecting the complexity of its dynamic nature. While long-term trends at continental and global scales have been analysed primarily using Landsat observations6–8 , substantial knowledge gaps persist. Key aspects of lake-surface-extent dynamics, particularly the role of seasonality, are not yet fully understood owing to the limited temporal resolution of existing datasets4,5 .

For example, a recent analysis of seasonal water-level variations using ICESat-2 data between October 2018 and July 2020 (ref. 22) involved subtracting minimum from maximum water levels over a 22-month period. Although this study offered a notable improvement in scientific understanding, it was subject to limited observations, which introduced uncertainty and may have conflated seasonal with interannual variations. The restricted study period also constrained the analysis of the relative influence of natural and anthropogenic factors on seasonality, potentially overlooking its broader role in shaping total variation. In addition, this brief timeframe precluded a comprehensive examination of low-water extremes (for example, in defining a historic baseline) induced by seasonality, which substantially impact ecosystem adaptability, hydrological cycle stability and methane emissions12,19,23,24.

Other previous efforts to map global surface water bodies at high temporal resolution using Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) observations were limited by coarse spatial resolution $( > 2 5 0 \mathrm { m } ) ^ { 2 5 }$ , whereas attempts to address data gaps in Landsat images faced inherent limitations such as low temporal resolution and the Landsat 7 scan-line-corrector failure26,27. The fundamental trade-off between spatial and temporal resolutions in single-source satellite observations has driven the development of spatiotemporal fusion algorithms. Although multisource approaches using Sentinel-2 show promise, their utility is constrained by limited data availability, particularly pre- $\cdot 2 0 1 5 ^ { 2 8 }$ . Methods fusing MODIS and Landsat data have shown success in small-scale experiments but struggle with global scalability owing to efficiency constraints and dependence on gap-free Landsat images29,30. These limitations underscore the need for advanced spatiotemporal fusion methods to enable comprehensive monitoring of global lake-surface-extent dynamics.

To address these gaps, we developed a U-Net model with a spatial attention mechanism (Methods) to produce an effective spatiotemporal fusion of MODIS observations and the Landsat-derived Global Surface Water (GSW) dataset7 at a global scale. Recent advances, such as the dataset produced by the Global Land Analysis and Discovery (GLAD) laboratory, have achieved excellent accuracy $94 \%$ and $9 6 \%$ for user’s accuracy and producer’s accuracy, respectively) compared with GSW $9 0 \%$ and $9 5 \%$ ) validated against 116 RapidEye images $( 2 0 \times 2 0 \mathrm { k m } ) ^ { 3 1 }$ However, we used GSW for its readily accessible occurrence and recurrence metrics with longer temporal coverage, which enhances our spatiotemporal fusion approach. We created the first dataset of monthly, gap-free time series for approximately 1.4 million lakes globally, spanning from 2001 to 2023. Our dataset achieves accuracy comparable to the current state-of-the-art dataset7 (Extended Data Figs. 1 and 2) with median user’s accuracy and producer’s accuracy of $9 3 \%$ and $96 \%$ respectively, when validated against GSW, while remarkedly reducing the missing-data rate (Extended Data Figs. 3 and 4 and Methods). Here ‘lake’ encompasses both natural lakes and reservoirs as defined by LakeATLAS for convenience, and ‘surface extent’ refers specifically to open water surfaces, acknowledging the limitation of visible and near-infrared electromagnetic waves in penetrating canopy cover.

Using this dataset, we systematically analysed the surface-extent dynamics of approximately 1.4 million lakes from 2001 to 2023, decomposing total variations into 3 components: long-term trends, interannual variability and seasonality (Fig. 1). Long-term trends represent changes in the average lake surface extent18 (Fig. 1b), and interannual variability refers to annual deviations from the mean state during the study period (Fig. 1c). Together, these components constitute non-seasonal variation. Seasonality captures the dynamics of ephemeral water bodies32 (Fig. 1d). We quantified long-term trends using the linear slopes of STL-decomposed (seasonal and trend decomposition using loess) trend terms33, non-seasonal variations through the standard deviations of annual means, and seasonality through intra-annual standard deviations (Methods). We avoided using the STL-decomposed seasonal components, as the method often classifies extremes as residuals, potentially misrepresenting the variability of lake surface extent. Comparing these components’ relative contributions to total variation reveals seasonality as the dominant factor shaping spatiotemporal dynamics, with seasonality-induced low-water extremes capable of counteracting long-term expansion and intensifying contraction.

# Seasonality of lake surface extent

To evaluate the impact of seasonality on overall lake-surface-extent variations, we compared its contribution with that of non-seasonal variability, which encompasses both long-term trends and interannual variability. Non-seasonal variability was quantified as the standard deviation of the annual means over a 23-year period from 2001 to 2023, and seasonality was measured as the average intra-annual standard deviation of monthly lake surface extents over the same period (Methods). During 2001–2023, the global average lake surface extent, excluding the Caspian Sea as an outlier, was $2 , 4 0 5 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ , with a total seasonality of $6 8 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 } ( 2 . 8 \% )$ and non-seasonal variability of $7 8 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 } \left( 3 . 2 \% \right)$ . The remainder represents the stable component, which remained consistently inundated over the entire period. The slightly larger non-seasonal variability stems from a few large lakes with notable long-term trends, such as the South Aral Sea, which shrank by $8 5 0 \mathsf { k m } ^ { 2 } \mathsf { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ and showed average seasonal variation and non-seasonal variation of $1 { , } 4 0 0 \mathsf { k m } ^ { 2 }$ and $5 { , } 5 0 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ , respectively.

To account for these large lakes with substantial long-term trends and to better quantify the impact of seasonality on individual lakes, we defined the relative contribution of seasonality as the ratio of the average seasonal variation to the total variation (Methods). Lakes with a relative seasonality contribution greater than $50 \%$ were categorized as seasonality dominated. Our analysis indicates that seasonality predominantly influences global lake-surface-extent variations, with seasonality-dominated lakes making up $6 6 \%$ of the total average area and approximately $60 \%$ of all lakes (Fig. 2a). This is consistent across all lake size categories, for which the median relative contribution of seasonality exceeds $50 \%$ (Fig. 2c). Notably, all size categories, except for the smallest lakes $( 0 . 0 3 \mathrm { - } 1 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 } , 5 3 \% )$ , have median relative contributions above $5 5 \%$ , with some large lakes $_ { ( 1 0 0 - 1 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { k m ^ { 2 } } ) }$ ) exceeding $60 \%$ . It is important to note that the dominance of seasonality does not preclude the presence of long-term trends or interannual variations; some lakes do show notable non-seasonal variations6,18. However, the magnitude of these non-seasonal changes is often overshadowed by seasonal variations, meaning that seasonality primarily dictates lake surface extent over non-seasonal variations. For example, among the 491,362 lakes with significant long-term trends $( P { < } 0 . 0 5 )$ , the median relative seasonality contribution is $54 \%$ .

The prominence of seasonality in global lake-surface-extent variations is further underscored by the magnitude of the relative contribution of seasonality. When this contribution exceeds $60 \%$ , seasonality is 1.5 times non-seasonal variation; at $6 6 . 7 \%$ , it is double. Most latitudinal bands show median relative seasonality contributions exceeding $60 \%$ , with many surpassing $65 \%$ or even $70 \%$ (Fig. 2b). This highlights the importance of understanding seasonal dynamics for numerous applications, including integrating lakes into global greenhouse gas budgets13,14,19. Regions with high relative contributions of seasonality are those that exhibit high total surface-extent variability, such as the eastern USA, parts of Europe, the Amazon, Southeast Asia, China and India (Extended Data Fig. 5), reinforcing the crucial role of seasonality in global lake-surface-extent dynamics. In lakes where seasonality dominates and total relative variations are moderate to small, its influence remains important, particularly in shaping wet–dry dynamics of the littoral zone. These zones, although comprising a small fraction of the total lake area, are crucial for benthic primary production, fish habitat11 and active carbon exchange, particularly in northern regions such as the Canadian Shield and Siberia34. Consequently, changes in these littoral zones have the potential to alter lacustrine biochemistry, ecology and carbon cycles substantially.

Exceptions to the global dominance of seasonality are observed in regions such as Australia, Central Asia, the Tibetan Plateau and the Arctic, where non-seasonal variations are greater than seasonality. These exceptions are driven by specific regional factors: Australia’s southeast experienced the Millennium Drought from 2001 to 2009 (ref. 35), followed by partial recovery7,24; Central Asia has seen extensive lake loss owing to human activities7 ; the Tibetan Plateau has shown consistent lake expansion since 2000, primarily owing to changing precipitation patterns and glacial melt36; and Arctic lakes have been diminishing in size owing to rising temperatures and thawing permafrost37.

The dominance of seasonality shows strong spatial correlations with human residence. Using BasinAtlas level-06 subbasins38, we classified a subbasin as seasonality dominated if the median relative contribution of seasonality for its lakes exceeded $50 \%$ . Our analysis of population distribution38,39 (Methods) suggests that $9 3 \%$ of the global population (over 6 billion people) live in seasonality-dominated basins, with $51 \%$ (over 3 billion people) residing in areas where seasonality is at least twice as strong as non-seasonal variations (Fig. 2d). These findings highlight the need for high-temporal-resolution monitoring of lake dynamics to detect changes in the magnitude and timing of intra-annual lake seasonal cycles. In seasonality-dominated basins, changes in seasonal variations can have a much larger impact on local ecosystem health than long-term trends or interannual variations23,40, potentially affecting human livelihoods and ecosystem services41. The prevalence of seasonality in lake systems inhabited by most of the global population underscores its critical role in water-resource management, ecosystem services and climate regulation at both local and global scales.

The spatial correlation between human settlement patterns and lake-surface-extent seasonality reflects interactions between anthropogenic activities and hydrological cycles. Globally, 6,750 reservoirs and human-regulated lakes (as classified by LakeATLAS15) show a higher median relative seasonality contribution $( 6 5 \% )$ compared with 1,373,942 natural lakes $( 5 3 \% )$ , with the latter predominantly located in sparsely populated regions, such as the Canadian Shield42. Natural lakes in densely populated subbasins $_ { \cdot \ge 1 1 }$ people per $ { \mathrm { k m } } ^ { 2 } ,$ ) show relative seasonality contributions exceeding $65 \%$ (Extended Data Fig. 6), suggesting notable indirect impacts of human activities within these basins. This pattern indicates both direct anthropogenic modification of surface-water periodicity in reservoirs and indirect effects on natural lakes within the same basin.

# Changes in seasonality

To analyse global changes in the seasonality of lake surface extent (2001–2023), we calculated linear trends of 23 annual standard deviations for each lake. To ensure that the results were not disproportionately influenced by a few very large lakes, we computed relative changes as the ratio of these linear trends to the average seasonality (Methods). Our results reveal more pronounced geographical heterogeneity in seasonality changes (Fig. 3a) compared with long-term trends (Fig. 4e), probably owing to seasonality’s shorter temporal scales and greater sensitivity to climate change and natural variability.

Despite the observed heterogeneity, $53 \%$ of lakes showed increased seasonality in surface extent, amounting to a total increase of $5 , 8 0 0 \mathsf { k m } ^ { 2 }$ per decade (approximately equivalent to the area of 5.5 Three Gorges reservoirs, one of the world’s largest hydropower facilities). Conversely, $4 7 \%$ of lakes experienced a decrease in seasonality, totalling a reduction of $6 { , } 9 0 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ per decade (roughly 6.5 Three Gorges reservoirs). This trend generally persists across different lake size classes, with increasing trends in seasonality being more common, except among very large lakes $( > 5 , 0 0 0 \mathsf { k m } ^ { 2 } )$ , among which $57 \%$ show decreasing seasonality. The overall smaller increase compared with the decrease can be attributed to the geographical variability in seasonality distribution and changes. Although lakes in regions such as Siberia and Europe mostly show increasing relative changes, their baseline average seasonality is lower than that in regions with overall decreasing trends, such as the northeastern USA and Central Asia. This disparity in baseline seasonality amplifies the global impact of decreasing trends, despite fewer lakes experiencing them.

The geographical heterogeneity of changes in lake-surface-extent seasonality reflects complex responses to climate change and variability, driven by factors such as alterations in thermal conditions, precipitation patterns and permafrost degradation43,44. To better understand this heterogeneity, we categorized lakes on the basis of permafrost distribution45 (Fig. 3b) and the average aridity index of their upstream watersheds38,46 (Fig. 3d). Lakes located in regions with substantial permafrost coverage (permafrost continuity $5 5 0 \%$ ) generally show more pronounced increasing trends in seasonality $( > 2 . 5 \%$ per decade) compared with areas with little or no permafrost (Fig. 3b). In contrast, lakes in arid climates tend to experience decreasing trends in seasonality $( > 5 \%$ per decade), whereas those in humid climates predominantly show increasing trends (Fig. 3d). Specific regional patterns are evident in densely populated areas with notable seasonality changes, such as the eastern USA, India and Europe (Fig. 3c). For instance, in India, 6,190 lakes located to the east of $7 8 ^ { \circ }$ E have a median relative seasonality change of $- 4 . 1 \%$ per decade, whereas 4,690 lakes to the west of $7 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { \bf E }$ show a median increase of $3 . 7 \%$ per decade.

At the basin scale, increases in the seasonality of lake surface extent, as indicated by changes in annual standard deviations, suggest heightened variability in regional hydrological cycles22. Globally, $53 \%$ of the global population resides in basins with decreasing seasonality trends, whereas $4 7 \%$ live in areas experiencing intensifying seasonality. Notably, $2 2 \%$ of the global population is located in basins (BasinATLAS level-06 subbasins) in which the median relative increase in seasonality exceeds $5 \%$ per decade, such as the eastern USA, Europe and Southeast

Australia (Fig. 3c). This intensification may challenge the adaptability and resilience of lacustrine ecosystems by destabilizing littoral benthic resources, which are crucial for many aquatic species11. Such changes could have substantial impacts on human livelihoods and necessitate increased focus on understanding and mitigating these effects.

# Seasonality-induced extremes

To highlight the importance of seasonality for all lakes, including those with notable long-term trends, we analysed both long-term trends and seasonality-induced low-water extremes in lake surface extent. For long-term trends, we employed robust STL decomposition to separate the effects of seasonality and calculated linear trends from the STL-decomposed trend components for the period from 2001 to 2023, with statistical significance determined using the Mann–Kendall test47. To isolate seasonality-induced low-water extremes, we conducted quantile analysis48 on the detrended time series (obtained by subtracting annual means; Methods). By comparing the magnitudes of 23-year long-term trends, average seasonal variations and seasonalityinduced low-water extremes, we found that these extreme events can either counterbalance long-term expansions or intensify long-term contractions.

Globally, lakes showed a net long-term shrinkage of 15, $. 0 0 0 \mathsf { k m } ^ { 2 }$ per decade from 2001 to 2023 (Fig. 4a), aligning with previous studies on long-term water-storage changes in thousands of large natural lakes and reservoirs6 . Our analysis of over 1.4 million lakes further reveals a heterogeneous global pattern (Fig. 4b–e): $53 \%$ of lakes experienced a shrinkage rate of $\mathbf { \delta } 3 0 0 \mathbf { k m } ^ { 2 }$ per decade, whereas $4 7 \%$ expanded at a rate of $2 3 , 3 0 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ per decade. The shrinkage was primarily associated with natural lakes, whereas both natural lakes and reservoirs contributed to the observed expansion. Within a given timeframe, seasonality-induced low-water extremes can counteract long-term expansions or intensify long-term shrinkages in lake surface extent. We quantified these extremes as the maximum deviation from the corresponding annual mean and assessed their impact using a relative impact metric. This metric, calculated as a negative value to reflect the drying effect, is defined as the ratio of the magnitude of seasonality-induced low-water extremes to the absolute sum of this magnitude, average seasonality and 23-year long-term changes (Methods and Fig. 5). A value below $- 5 0 \%$ indicates that the water loss caused by seasonality-induced low-water extremes exceeds the combined magnitude of regular seasonal variations and 23-year long-term changes. For expanding lakes, this suggests that long-term surface extent increases can be fully offset by seasonality-induced low-water extremes (Fig. 5b). Conversely, for shrinking lakes, it implies that long-term reductions can be effectively doubled by these extremes (Fig. 5b).

Seasonality-induced low-water extremes that offset expansions and exacerbate shrinkages are globally prevalent (Fig. 5a), except in regions with notable long-term trends (for example, Central Asia and the Tibetan Plateau; Fig. 4e) or minimal seasonality (for example, eastern Alaska and northeastern Siberia). Even for lakes with significant long-term changes, the impact of these extremes remains substantial. Among 207,746 significantly expanding lakes $( P < 0 . 0 5 , 2 0 0 1 – 2 0 2 3 )$ , the total long-term increase and average seasonality were $4 8 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ , with seasonality-induced low-water extremes amounting to $2 9 . 5 0 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ . For 262,627 significantly shrinking lakes, the combined total of long-term decrease and average seasonality was $9 6 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ , with extremes accounting for $5 4 , 4 0 0 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ . Over $4 2 \%$ of significantly shrinking lakes experienced at least a doubling of their contraction owing to these extremes, whereas more than $45 \%$ of significantly expanding lakes had their expansions completely offset. Regions such as the eastern USA and India illustrate cases in which expansion is offset, potentially draining both newly gained and pre-existing areas. Conversely, regions such as Europe and eastern China show exacerbated shrinkage, heightening risks by intensifying the long-term decline multiple times.

The impact of seasonality-induced low-water extremes is nearly independent of the direction and magnitude of long-term trends (Fig. 5c,d). Lakes with pronounced expansions or contractions show similar medians and distributions of relative impact, except for those with small relative long-term trends $( < 0 . 1 \%$ per decade), which show greater susceptibility to these extremes. However, the magnitude of these impacts correlates with trends in seasonality. Lakes experiencing intensified seasonality suffer more severe seasonality-induced low-water extremes, with all groups showing a median relative contribution below $- 5 0 \%$ (ranging from $- 5 1 . 6 \%$ to $- 6 0 . 0 \%$ ; Fig. 5c).

High-water extremes mirror the effects of low-water extremes, counteracting long-term shrinkage and amplifying expansions in lakes (Extended Data Fig. 7). Approximately $3 9 \%$ of significantly shrinking lakes $( P < 0 . 0 5 , 2 0 0 1 – 2 0 2 3 )$ experience complete compensation during high-water extremes, whereas about $4 4 \%$ of significantly expanding lakes show doubled expansion. Lakes with increased seasonality face more severe high-water extremes, with all groups showing median relative contributions above $50 \%$ $( 5 0 . 2 \substack { - 5 8 . 3 \% } )$ ). The frequency of both low- and high-water extremes has changed between 2001–2011 and 2013–2023 (Extended Data Fig. 8). Globally, seasonality-induced low-water extremes have increased, particularly in the eastern USA and Europe (Extended Data Fig. 8a), whereas high-water extreme changes show more complex spatial patterns (Extended Data Fig. 8b).

# Discussion

Although long-term trends in lake surface extent and water storage have been extensively studied7,18,26,27, crucial aspects such as seasonality have often been overlooked. This is primarily owing to the inherent trade-offs between the spatial and temporal resolution of single-source satellite observations7,25 and the absence of effective spatiotemporal fusion techniques30. Our study addresses these challenges by employing deep learning to achieve monthly, gap-free monitoring of surface extent variations at a $_ { 3 0 - \mathsf { m } }$ resolution for approximately 1.4 million lakes worldwide from 2001 to 2023. This dataset allows a comprehensive analysis of long-term trends, interannual variability and seasonality in global lake surface extent, filling a critical knowledge gap in our understanding of lake dynamics.

Our findings reveal the predominant influence of seasonality on lake-surface-extent dynamics globally and highlight the notable impact of seasonality-induced extremes in counteracting and intensifying long-term changes. Lakes dominated by seasonal fluctuations account for $6 6 \%$ of the total average surface area and comprise around $60 \%$ of all lakes. Lakes located in more densely populated regions show higher degrees of seasonality dominance, suggesting potential anthropogenic alterations to their intra-annual cycles. Across most latitudes, the median magnitude of seasonality is at least 1.5 times non-seasonal variations. Although some lakes show larger long-term trends than seasonality, our analysis indicates that the effects of seasonality-induced extremes can exceed the combined impact of long-term trends and typical seasonal fluctuations. These widespread and notable seasonality-induced extremes have profound implications for greenhouse gas emissions, ecosystem health and human livelihoods48. Abrupt and drastic contractions in surface extent can expose benthic sediments, potentially leading to substantial methane releases19. These extremes also modify local climate49 and challenge the resilience of aquatic ecosystems, potentially destabilizing lacustrine food webs, altering the distribution of dominant primary producers and precipitating critical, potentially irreversible ecosystem regime shifts12,23,24,50, thereby affecting human well-being. The dominant role of seasonality and the substantial influence of these extremes underscore the need for comprehensive monitoring and understanding of seasonality, which is essential for enhancing lacustrine ecosystem protection10–12, improving freshwater resource management3 and refining global greenhouse gas budget estimates13,14.

Our study represents an important advancement by revealing previously obscured global seasonal dynamics in lake surface extent, demonstrating their critical importance and showcasing the potential of multisource remote sensing. We demonstrate the effectiveness of deep-learning techniques in the spatiotemporal fusion of multisource remote-sensing data, overcoming the limitations of single-source satellite observations. Future improvements could involve integrating additional high-quality inputs, such as ice- and snow-cover maps, to achieve more accurate extraction of liquid open water. As computational power and storage capacity continue to grow, higher temporal resolution and the fusion of data from an increasing number of satellite sources will become possible. This advancement could lead to even more comprehensive datasets with higher temporal resolution, capturing a wider range of inland water bodies, including rivers.

# Methods

Our methodology consists of four main stages (Extended Data Fig. 9a). First, we updated the lake boundaries provided by LakeATLAS using the GLAKES dataset, organizing the approximately 1.4 million lakes into 60 groups on the basis of the BasinATLAS level-02 subbasins. Second, we developed training datasets for each basin, constructed a U-Net model and applied spatiotemporal fusion techniques to create monthly maps of lake surface extent with minimal data gaps. Third, we extracted and analysed the monthly time series of surface extent for each individual lake. Finally, we validated our results by comparing them with altimetry-based water levels and data from the GSW dataset, excluding regions in which GSW data were not valid.

# Determining lake boundaries

To evaluate the surface-extent dynamics for each lake, we first determined their boundaries. We primarily used LakeATLAS, a subset of HydroATLAS38, which includes 1,427,688 natural lakes and reservoirs with surface areas larger than $0 . 1 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ . However, some lakes—such as Selin Co in the Tibetan Plateau—showed substantial expansion beyond their LakeATLAS-defined boundaries, leading to an underestimation of surface extent. To reduce this effect, we updated the LakeATLAS boundaries with the GLAKES dataset18, which defines lake outlines on the basis of the maximum extents observed between 1984 and 2021 in the GSW dataset7 . This update reduced the total number of lakes to 1,401,802, as some neighbouring lakes have merged owing to surface expansion. The minimum size of lakes after the update is $0 . 0 3 \mathrm { k m } ^ { 2 }$ , equal to that in the GLAKES dataset. Furthermore, to account for ephemeral dynamics potentially missed by GLAKES and those of lakes present in LakeATLAS but absent from GLAKES, we applied a size- and topography-dependent buffer to all lake boundaries (equations (1)–(3)). Although this approach enhances the capture of ephemeral variations, it cannot address the exclusion of ephemeral lakes not initially present in LakeATLAS, a limitation that awaits future improvements in lake typology datasets.

# Spatiotemporal fusion using U-Net

In surface-water mapping, spatiotemporal fusion combines data from different satellite sources over time. It involves breaking down large, low-resolution image pixels (for example, $5 0 0 \mathrm { m } ,$ ) into smaller, more detailed ones (for example, $3 0 \mathrm { m }$ ) using high-resolution satellite data. Optical sensors onboard satellites measure surface reflectance, which is determined by the fractions and electromagnetic properties of objects within each pixel. Assuming a one-to-one relationship, fractions can be calculated as the inverse of pixel reflectance (equation (4)). This concept forms the theoretical foundation for earlier studies51. By using these fractions and the size of the fine pixels (for example, $3 0 \mathrm { m }$ ), we determine how many unmixed pixels there are for both water and non-water categories. Their spatial distribution is then determined using information from neighbouring coarse pixels and additional sources (equation (5)), allowing us to extract water masks from these unmixed pixels (equation (6)).

$$
\mathbf { r } _ { \mathrm { p i x e l } } { = } F ( \mathbf { p } | \mathbf { \ B } ) \iff \mathbf { p } { = } F ^ { - 1 } ( \mathbf { r } _ { \mathrm { p i x e l } } | \mathbf { \ B } ) ,
$$

$$
\begin{array} { r } {  { \boldsymbol { X } } = G (  { \mathbf { p } } , R _ { \mathrm { p i x e l } } ^ { \prime } , \mathbf { a } ) = G (  { \boldsymbol { F } } ^ { - 1 } (  { \mathbf { r } } _ { \mathrm { p i x e l } } | \ \mathbf { \beta } ) , R _ { \mathrm { p i x e l } } ^ { \prime } , \mathbf { a } ) , } \end{array}
$$

$$

The term a represents additional information to aid in the unmixing process. $Y _ { \mathrm { w a t e r } }$ is the matrix of water masks at fine spatial resolution. The functions $F ( \cdot ) , G ( \cdot ) , H ( \cdot )$ and $K ( \cdot )$ represent the operations defined in each equation, with $K ( \cdot )$ producing unmixed water masks from reflectances of coarse pixels and additional data. We used deep learning to model this complex relationship, training on monthly MODIS–GSW image pairs to implement the spatiotemporal fusion52.

Deep learning has demonstrated success across various scientific fields53–55, including satellite remote sensing56–58. U-Net, one of the most successful deep-learning models for image segmentation59,60, is particularly well suited to our goal of extracting dynamics in lake surface extent. It has been effectively applied in various remote-sensing applications, such as tree crown extraction61, lake and reservoir classification18, and urban development monitoring62. U-Net’s encoder– decoder architecture with skip connections is particularly suited for our spatiotemporal fusion approach, enabling simultaneous extraction of spectral and spatial patterns. The encoder’s convolutional layers learn optimal nonlinear combinations of spectral bands to determine subpixel water content while extracting hierarchical spatial patterns. Skip connections preserve critical spatial details by directly linking encoder layers to corresponding decoder layers. The decoder synthesizes fine-scale spatial features from skip connections with high-level abstract features processed through the U-shaped pathway. Finally, upsampling convolutions in the decoder enable precise spatial reconstruction at finer resolutions (for example, ${ 3 0 }  { \mathrm { m } } ,$ , effectively distributing water extent information to achieve spatiotemporal fusion.

To our knowledge, U-Net has not been applied to inland-water-body mapping at continental and global scales owing to the challenges of processing the vast amounts of remote-sensing data. We leveraged geospatial-processing-oriented cloud computing platforms, such as Google Earth Engine63, and local high-performance-computing clusters to apply U-Net models with spatial attention mechanisms (Extended Data Fig. 9b) to terabytes of Terra MODIS data and the GSW dataset. By training on the highly accurate GSW dataset7 , our U-Net model learned to unmix coarse MODIS pixels $( 5 0 0 \mathsf { m } )$ into water masks at a $_ { 3 0 \cdot \mathsf { m } }$ resolution, equivalent to that of Landsat observations. Our model used six MODIS electromagnetic spectrum bands (blue, green, red, near-infrared and two shortwave infrared) and two GSW statistics (water occurrence and recurrence during 1984–2021) as input to generate water mask maps. We incorporated these two statistics into the input features to address the challenge of strong reliance on gap-free Landsat observations64,65, allowing our model to focus on areas in which water is likely to occur through the spatial attention mechanism.

The first step in developing U-Net models was preparing high-quality, representative training data. To ensure representativeness, we constructed separate U-Net models for each BasinATLAS level-2 subbasin, balancing spatial coverage and model generalizability. To ensure data

# Article

quality, we preprocessed MODIS data, resampling it to $_ { 3 0 \mathfrak { m } }$ , masking cloud pixels using quality flags and aggregating it to monthly medians to align with GSW’s monthly history product. This monthly median aggregation serves as both a technical approach and a definition of monthly lake surface extent, effectively filtering extreme observations while capturing water conditions under periods that are not completely cloud contaminated. The MODIS and GSW data were then reprojected to a custom Lambert azimuthal equal-area projection for each subbasin, preserving areas and minimizing distortions for accurate pixel unmixing and surface extent calculation. We randomly sampled $1 2 8 \times 1 2 8$ pixel kernels from the combined MODIS–GSW data, excluding samples containing non-valid labels (snow, ice, cloud or sensor failure)7 to ensure binary water and non-water classification. These samples were split into training, validation and test sets, distributed uniformly across spatial and temporal dimensions. The sizes of the training, validation and test sets were selected to meet the requirements of U-Net60, mitigating overfitting risks.

Next, we used the intersection over union (IoU) as our loss function during training, constraining the sampling regions to reduce the influence of data imbalance. Data imbalance, in which non-water labels dominate, is a common issue in our study, given that inland water bodies cover only a small fraction of the land surface. If not managed appropriately, this imbalance can greatly affect U-Net model performance. IoU, defined as the ratio of the intersection to the union of model-predicted masks and true water masks, is inherently insensitive to data imbalance as it focuses solely on water labels. In addition, we constrained random sampling regions to the minimum bounding envelopes of lake boundaries, increasing the proportion of water labels in the training data. These approaches mitigate the impact of data imbalance on model performance and sampling efficiency.

Finally, we trained the U-Net models, carefully selecting the number of epochs for each basin’s model to minimize the risks of overfitting and underfitting. Despite high-quality training data and addressed data imbalance, U-Net models can still be susceptible to these issues if the number of training epochs is not optimal. By analysing IoU curves on both training and validation sets, we identified the optimal epoch as the point at which training IoU plateaued and validation IoU stopped increasing. This approach allowed us to select the iteration just before the onset of overfitting, ensuring the optimal performance and generalizability of our U-Net models. This approach also helps mitigate uncertainties that might be propagated from GSW to our monthly lake-surface-extent maps. The overall high accuracy of GSW ensures that the majority of our training samples are reliable, with only minimal noise arising from the omission and commission errors of GSW. This careful manual selection of training epochs ensures that our U-Net models effectively learn generalizable patterns for unmixing and water classification while avoiding overfitting to noise in the training data.

Our U-Net models processed inputs of $1 2 8 \times 1 2 8 \times 8$ (width $\times$ height $\times$ channel) at a $_ { 3 0 \cdot \mathsf { m } }$ spatial resolution. For each basin, we preprocessed the MODIS data and GSW water occurrence and recurrence according to our training data protocol, combining them into monthly 8-band rasters at a nominal $_ { 3 0 \cdot \mathsf { m } }$ resolution. These rasters were then sliced into $1 2 8 \times 1 2 8 \times 8$ tiles with 32-pixel overlaps along the width or height dimensions to mitigate edge effects. The tiled inputs were fed into corresponding U-Net models, which produced $1 2 8 \times 1 2 8 \times 1$ water mask tiles. These water mask tiles were subsequently mosaiced into a single water distribution map, from which we extracted the monthly surface extents for individual lakes.

In summary, we implemented spatiotemporal fusion of MODIS and GSW data for lake-surface-extent mapping by employing deep learning and combining image-pair-based and spatial-unmixing-based methods, both key paradigms in spatiotemporal fusion52. U-Net models with spatial attention mechanisms were trained to learn how to unmix coarse pixels and extract open water bodies, such as natural lakes and reservoirs, guided by high-spatial-resolution water occurrence and recurrence statistics from GSW. We processed terabytes of MODIS and GSW data, generating nearly gap-free global monthly maps of lake surface extent from 2001 to 2023. This comprehensive dataset enabled systematic analysis of surface extent dynamics for approximately 1.4 million lakes worldwide.

# Extraction and post-processing

We derived monthly time series of surface extents for about 1.4 million lakes from the spatiotemporal fusion dataset using a rasterizing-vector approach on a high-performance-computing cluster. To ensure accurate lake identification, buffered lake boundaries from LakeATLAS were rasterized, with pixels assigned unique lake IDs, or marked as ‘none’ if not inside any lake boundary. A pixel was classified as part of a lake if its centre point lay within the lake’s defined boundary. The count of water pixels within each lake boundary was then determined by overlaying these rasterized lake boundaries with monthly water masks. The use of the Lambert azimuthal equal-area projection, which preserves area, enabled precise calculation of lake surface extent by multiplying the number of water pixels by the pixel area $( 9 0 0 \mathsf { m } ^ { 2 } )$ .

Although spatiotemporal fusion was optimized to minimize data gaps and enhance accuracy, post-processing was essential to account for two key issues: ice coverage and persistent cloud contamination. The first issue arose from the undefined behaviour of our model for ice-covered pixels, as they were classified as non-valid in the GSW dataset and excluded from the training samples. The second issue stemmed from cloud cover in Terra MODIS data, which could lead to occasional gaps and spikes in the time series of lake surface extent. To ensure the accuracy and reliability of the time series, we applied tailored post-processing strategies to resolve these challenges.

For ice-affected months, during which lake surface extent was uncertain, owing to the exclusion of ice pixels from the model, we adopted a proxy approach using temperature data. Although incorporating high-quality ice labels into the model training would have been ideal, the GSW dataset’s treatment of ice pixels as non-valid (often mixed with cloud cover) and the absence of a reliable global lake ice dataset made this infeasible. Instead, we used the Simstrat mode $\mathsf { I } ^ { 6 6 , 6 7 } - \mathsf { a }$ one-dimensional lake simulation tool—to estimate ice cover on the basis of the strong correlation between lake freezing and temperatures. Hourly simulations of temperature and ice cover were conducted for each of the approximately 1.4 million lakes, and daily averages were calculated. A lake was deemed frozen for a given month if it was ice-covered for more than $10 \%$ of days. For these frozen months, lake surface extent was set to match the most recent non-frozen month, under the assumption that lake extent remains static during periods of ice cover.

To address cloud contamination, which could cause spikes in the time series, we developed a two-step correction process. First, we quantified the cloud-contamination ratio for each lake on a monthly basis using Terra MODIS observations. If the cloud-contamination ratio exceeded $5 \%$ , the lake surface extent for that month was flagged as unreliable. For months during which the cloud-contamination ratio ranged between $0 . 1 \%$ and $5 \%$ , we applied local-outlier-factor detection to identify potential cloud-induced outliers. Surface-extent values identified as outliers within this contamination range were also flagged as unreliable. In both cases, cloud-affected data points were replaced using two-sided linear interpolation, using the nearest preceding and subsequent cloud-free months as reference points. This approach remarkedly reduced the impact of cloud-related anomalies in the time series, ensuring a more accurate representation of lake-surface-extent dynamics.

# Quantifying lake-surface-extent dynamics

We decomposed the total variation in lake surface extent into three key components: long-term trends, interannual variability and seasonality (Fig. 1), which we expressed mathematically as follows:

$$
A _ { i , j , k } = A _ { i } + \tau _ { i } ( j ) + \theta _ { i , j } \xi _ { j } + \eta _ { i , j } \epsilon _ { k } ,
$$

where $i , j$ and $k$ represent lake ID, year (from 2001) and month, respectively. $A _ { i , j , k }$ is the surface extent of lake i in month $k$ of year j (Fig. 1a), and $A _ { i }$ represents the lake’s initial extent at the start of the study. $\tau _ { i } ( j )$ denotes the long-term change for lake i from 2001 to year j (Fig. 1b). Interannual variability is captured by $\theta _ { i , j } \xi _ { j }$ (Fig. 1c), where $\theta _ { i , j }$ is a scaling factor and $\xi _ { j }$ is a zero-mean, unit-variance random variable. Seasonality is represented by $\eta _ { i , j } \varepsilon _ { k }$ (Fig. 1d), where $\eta _ { i , j }$ measures the magnitude of seasonality, and $\varepsilon _ { k }$ is a zero-mean, unit-variance random variable corresponding to monthly oscillation. It is noted that $k$ does not correspond to a fixed calendar month, ensuring randomness in this definition.

To estimate the magnitude of seasonality $( \eta _ { i , j } )$ , we calculated the intra-annual standard deviation $\sigma _ { i , j }$ as follows:

$$
\sigma _ { i , j } = \sqrt { \sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } } = \sqrt { \frac { 1 } { K _ { i , j } } \sum _ { k } { ( A _ { i , j , k } - \overline { { A _ { i , j } } } ) ^ { 2 } } } = \sqrt { \frac { 1 } { K _ { i , j } } \sum _ { k } { \left( A _ { i , j , k } - \frac { 1 } { K _ { i , j } } \sum _ { k } { A _ { i , j , k } } \right) ^ { 2 } } } ,
$$

where $\overline { { A } } _ { i , j }$ is the annual mean surface extent for lake i in year j, and $K _ { i , j }$ is the number of non-frozen months for lake $i$ in year j. $\sigma _ { \ i , j } ^ { 2 }$ is the intra-annual variance and $\sigma _ { i , j }$ represents the standard deviation.

For the non-seasonal component, we used the standard deviation of annual means $\delta _ { i } ,$ as the estimator:

$$
{ \delta _ { i } } \mathrm { { = } } \sqrt { { \delta _ { i } } ^ { 2 } }  = \sqrt { \frac { 1 } { J } \sum _ { j } { ( \overline { { { A _ { i , j } } } } - \overline { { { A _ { i } } } } ) ^ { 2 } } } = \sqrt { \frac { 1 } { J } \sum _ { j } { \left( \overline { { { A _ { i , j } } } } - \frac { 1 } { J } \sum _ { j } { \overline { { { A _ { i , j } } } } } \right) ^ { 2 } } } ,
$$

where ${ \overline { { A _ { i } } } }$ is the mean area of lake i during 2001–2023, and $\delta _ { i } ^ { 2 }$ represents the variance of the annual means. $J$ is the total number of years.

We then calculated the relative contribution of seasonality to lake-surface-extent dynamics by comparing its magnitude with that of the non-seasonal component. This ratio of seasonality to total variation was expressed as:

$$
C _ { \mathrm { s e a s o n a l i t y } } = \frac { \overline { { \sigma } } _ { i } } { \overline { { \sigma } } _ { i } + \delta _ { i } } = \frac { \frac { 1 } { J } \sum _ { j } \sigma _ { i , j } } { \frac { 1 } { J } \sum _ { j } \sigma _ { i , j } + \delta _ { i } } ,
$$

where $C _ { \mathrm { s e a s o n a l i t y } }$ is the relative contribution of seasonality (Fig. 2), and $\textstyle { \overline { { \sigma } } } _ { i }$ is the mean intra-annual standard deviation of lake i from 2001–2023. Cseasonality ranges from $0 \%$ to $100 \%$ , with values above $50 \%$ indicating seasonality dominance.

We explored seasonality dominance across latitude gradients, lake size categories and population distribution. For population analysis, we aggregated individual lake $C _ { \mathrm { s e a s o n a l i t y } }$ to BasinATLAS Level-06 basins38 (11,371 basins with ≥1 lake), using median values. Population data were derived from 2010 census data from the Gridded Population of the World database39.

To analyse seasonality changes (2001–2023), we calculated linear trends of intra-annual standard deviations $( \sigma _ { i , j } )$ for each lake, expressing relative changes as the ratio to average seasonality $\overline { { \sigma } } _ { i }$ (Fig. 3). We examined these changes against population density, aridity index and permafrost distribution45. For population analysis, we used the same protocol as for the seasonality contribution, whereas the aridity index, defined as the ratio of mean annual precipitation to evapotranspiration in the upstream watershed of each lake, was categorized according to the United Nations Environment Programme classification scheme (hyperarid, arid, semi-arid, dry subhumid and humid68), and permafrost distribution45 was overlaid using LakeATLAS to classify lakes into five zones: continuous, discontinuous, sporadic, isolated patches and no permafrost.

Seasonality-induced extremes were identified and quantified by applying monthly varying 10th and 90th percentiles48 to detrended time series. Focusing on extremes that occurred between 2013 and 2023, we quantified their magnitude as the maximum deviation from corresponding annual means, analysing their potential impacts relative to long-term changes and regular seasonal oscillation. We used these maxima owing to their potential to cause catastrophic, non-recoverable consequences12,23,24. The long-term trend was calculated using the linear trend of the STL-decomposed trend term with its significance determined using the Mann–Kendall test47, whereas regular seasonal oscillation was represented by the average seasonality. Finally, we defined the relative impact of seasonality-induced extremes as:

$$
I _ { \mathrm { { l o w - w a t e r } } } = \frac { - { \operatorname* { m a x } } ( | A _ { i , j , \mathrm { l o w - w a t e r } } - \overline { { A _ { i , j } } } | ) } { { \operatorname* { m a x } } ( | A _ { i , j , \mathrm { l o w - w a t e r } } - \overline { { A _ { i , j } } } | ) + J \beta _ { \mathrm { i } } + \overline { { \sigma _ { i } } } } ,
$$

$$
I _ { \mathrm { h i g h - w a t e r } } = \frac { \operatorname* { m a x } ( | A _ { i , j , \mathrm { h i g h - w a t e r } } - \overline { { A } } _ { i , j } | ) } { \operatorname* { m a x } ( | A _ { i , j , \mathrm { h i g h - w a t e r } } - \overline { { A } } _ { i , j } | ) + J \beta _ { \mathrm { i } } + \overline { { \sigma _ { i } } } } ,
$$

where $I _ { \mathrm { l o w \mathrm { \cdot w a t e r } } }$ and $I _ { \mathrm { h i g h - w a t e r } }$ are the relative impacts of seasonalityinduced low-water extremes and high-water extremes, respectively. $A _ { i , j , \mathrm { l o w \cdot w a t e r } }$ and $A _ { i , j , \mathrm { h i g h - w a t e r } }$ are the lake surface extent during low-water extremes and high-water extremes, respectively. $\beta _ { i }$ is the annual longterm trend, and is multiplied by the number of years J to obtain total long-term changes during 2001–2023. These metrics capture the relative significance of seasonality-induced extremes compared with longterm changes and average seasonal fluctuations.

# $\pmb { \sigma } _ { i , j }$ as an optimal estimator

We quantified seasonality magnitude using the intra-annual standard deviation $\sigma _ { i , j } .$ . Its square, ${ \sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } } ,$ , known as ‘population variance’, applies the variance definition directly to the data using the full data size as the denominator (equation (8)). This contrasts with the ‘sample variance’, $S ^ { 2 }$ , which uses data size minus one as the denominator. Although ${ \sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } }$ is a biased estimator, we demonstrate that it has a lower mean square error (MSE) than the unbiased $S ^ { 2 }$ (equation (13)). This lower MSE indicates higher accuracy in quantifying seasonality using $\sigma _ { i , j } ,$ whereas its underestimated bias yields more conservative results, enhancing the robustness of our conclusions.

$$
S ^ { 2 } = \frac { 1 } { K _ { i , j } - 1 } \sum _ { k } \left( A _ { i , j , k } - \frac { 1 } { K _ { i , j } } \sum _ { k } A _ { i , j , k } \right) ^ { 2 } .
$$

An estimator’s bias is defined as $| E ( \theta ^ { \prime } ) - \theta |$ , where $\theta ^ { \prime }$ is the estimator and $\theta$ the true value. The variance of an estimator is $E [ ( \theta ^ { \prime } - E ( \theta ^ { \prime } ) ) ^ { 2 } ]$ . The MSE combines these metrics: MSE $=$ Variance + Bias2 . This relationship demonstrates that MSE is a superior metric for evaluating an estimator’s overall performance, as it assesses the total discrepancy between the estimator and the true value. With the additional assumption that $\varepsilon _ { k }$ follows an independent standard normal distribution, we can prove that regarding k, $A _ { i , j , k } - \overline { { A } } _ { i , j }$ follows a normal distribution that has a mean of zero and variance of $\eta _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } .$ :

$$
A _ { i , j , k } - \overline { { A } } _ { i , j } = \eta _ { i , j } \left( \epsilon _ { k } - \frac { 1 } { K _ { i , j } } \sum _ { k } \epsilon _ { k } \right) .
$$

According to Cochran’s theorem, $K _ { i , j } \sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } / \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 2 }$ and $( K _ { i , j } - 1 ) S ^ { 2 } / \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 2 }$ follow a χK 2 i j, −1 distribution. Using the properties of chi-square distribution, we derive:

$$
E \left( \frac { K _ { i , j } \sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } } { \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } } \right) = E ( \chi _ { K _ { i , j ^ { - 1 } } } ^ { 2 } ) = K _ { i , j } - 1 ,
$$

$$
\mathsf { V a r } \left( \frac { K _ { i , j } \sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } } { \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } } \right) = \mathsf { V a r } ( \boldsymbol { \chi } _ { K _ { i , j } - 1 } ^ { 2 } ) = 2 ( K _ { i , j } - 1 ) .
$$

From this, we calculate the expectation of $\sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 }$ as $\eta _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } ( K _ { i , j } - 1 ) / K _ { i , j }$ and its variance as $2 \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 4 } ( K _ { i , j } - 1 ) / K _ { i , j } ^ { 2 }$ . The MSE of ${ \sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } }$ is:

$$
\mathbf { M S E } _ { \sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } } = \left( \frac { K _ { i , j } - 1 } { K _ { i , j } } \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } - \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } \right) ^ { 2 } + \frac { 2 \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 4 } ( K _ { i , j } - 1 ) } { K _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } } = \frac { \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 4 } ( 2 K _ { i , j } - 1 ) } { K _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } } ,
$$

where the first term is the square of bias and the second term is the variance of $\overline { { \sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } } }$ . Similarly, we can derive the MSE of $S ^ { 2 }$ as follows:

$$
\mathsf { M S E } _ { S ^ { 2 } } = ( 0 ) ^ { 2 } + \frac { 2 \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 4 } } { ( K _ { i , j } - 1 ) } = \frac { 2 \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 4 } } { ( K _ { i , j } - 1 ) } ,
$$

where the bias of $S ^ { 2 }$ is 0 and its variance is $2 \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 4 } / ( K _ { i , j } - 1 )$ . We can further compare the magnitude of MSE of $S ^ { 2 }$ and $\sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 }$ , and prove that $\sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 }$ has a smaller MSE as follows:

$$
\mathsf { M S E } _ { \sigma _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } } - \mathsf { M S E } _ { S ^ { 2 } } = - \frac { ( 3 K _ { i , j } - 1 ) \eta _ { i , j } ^ { 4 } } { K _ { i , j } ^ { 2 } ( K _ { i , j } - 1 ) } < 0 , ( K _ { i , j } > 1 ) .
$$

# Data validation

We employed a comprehensive validation of our dataset, consisting of three complementary parts. First, we evaluated the performance of our U-Net model through partitioned training, validation and test sets. The binary classification accuracy consistently achieved a median of approximately $9 9 \%$ across all sets, whereas the median IoU reached 0.95 on the training sets and exceeded 0.80 on both the validation and test sets. These metrics indicate robust model performance59,60, with the moderate gap between training and validation/test IoU values suggesting effective generalization, without signs of underfitting or overfitting. The geographical distribution of model performance (Extended Data Fig. 3a,b) demonstrated reliability across diverse regions, particularly in lake-rich areas such as the Canadian Shield.

Second, we assessed classification accuracy using pixel-wise comparisons with the GSW dataset as reference. Within each BasinATLAS level-02 basin, we generated random samples inside buffered lake boundaries, extracted classification labels from our datasets and GSW, and calculated confusion matrices for both water and non-water classification. This approach adheres to best practices for assessing classification accuracy69, including spatially stratified random sampling by limiting samples to buffered lake boundaries and using reference data of higher accuracy than the current data being evaluated. Given our aim to fill monthly data gaps in GSW through spatiotemporal fusion, using GSW as reference data maintains logical consistency in our validation framework. It also enabled the generation of dense validation samples across broad spatial and temporal scales to ensure statistical robustness. For the water class, nearly all basins achieved high accuracy, with median values of $9 3 . 3 \%$ for user’s accuracy, $9 6 . 2 \%$ for producer’s accuracy and 0.94 for F1 score (Extended Data Fig. 3c–e). The high accuracy metrics suggest strong consistency between our MODIS-derived products and the Landsat-based GSW dataset.

Third, we directly compared our spatiotemporal fusion results with the GSW dataset for approximately 1.4 million lakes (Extended Data Figs. 1 and 2), focusing on monthly lake surface extent. To ensure a fair comparison, pixels that GSW classifies as non-valid were excluded. Nearly all basins achieved an $R ^ { 2 } \approx 1 . 0$ , and most data points closely aligned with the 1:1 diagonal, reflecting strong consistency between the datasets. Most basins have a percent bias (PBIAS) within $\pm 5 \%$ and absolute PBIAS smaller than $1 5 \%$ . Discrepancies were partially attributed to temporal mismatches, as GSW mosaics monthly results from multiple images taken on different dates. Some basins showed slight systematic underestimation, probably owing to the coarser spatial resolution of MODIS. Furthermore, a comparison with altimetry-based water-level data for 47 lakes on the Tibetan Plateau36 showed mean and median Spearman correlation coefficients of 0.85 and 0.92, respectively, further confirming the robustness of our dataset (Extended Data Fig. 10).

# Effectiveness of spatiotemporal fusion

We compared data gaps in GSW and our monthly lake-surface-extent maps to demonstrate the effectiveness of our spatiotemporal fusion (Extended Data Fig. 4). The comparison focused on regions within 1,401,802 buffered lake boundaries, evaluating two metrics: missing area (non-valid pixels within lake boundaries) and missing count (lakes with $5 5 \%$ missing area). This $5 \%$ threshold was selected as it introduces uncertainty comparable to total lake-surface-extent variability.

To prevent overestimation of GSW data gaps during winter periods, when GSW excludes images with large solar zenith angles whereas our study treats these observations as valid, we excluded lakes during their frozen months from the comparison. Even with this adjustment, GSW shows prevalent data gaps with median monthly missing area and count ratios of $3 3 . 9 \%$ and $5 9 . 8 \%$ throughout 2001–2023, respectively, compared with our study’s $1 . 2 \%$ and $1 . 8 \%$ . Across basins, GSW’s median averaged monthly missing area and count ratios reach $4 0 . 5 \%$ (Extended Data Fig. 4c) and $6 4 . 1 \%$ (Extended Data Fig. 4e), whereas our study has these ratios at $1 . 3 \%$ and $1 . 7 \%$ , with most regions approaching $0 \%$ (Extended Data Fig. 4d,f). These substantial improvements in data coverage, combined with high accuracy, demonstrate the effectiveness of our spatiotemporal fusion method.

Extended Data Fig. 9 | U-Net models with spatial attention enable accurate global lake mapping through spatiotemporal fusion. (a) Workflow of this study. First, lake boundaries from LakeATLAS15 were updated using GLAKES18. Basins from the BasinATLAS38 level-02 product that have almost no lakes were excluded. Second, training data were generated, and U-Net models were trained in each basin. Third, the monthly time series of surface extent was calculated, and its dynamics were analyzed for each lake. Finally, our data were validated against the GSW7 dataset, excluding areas with non-valid GSW data. Data preprocessing was implemented on GEE, and subsequent processing and computation were performed on an HPC cluster. (b) U-Net architecture with the spatial attention mechanism. The model features four encoder and decoder layers (maximum depth: 256), with spatial attention incorporated into the skip-connections. Input data include six MODIS bands (blue, green, red, near-infrared, and two shortwave infrared) along with two statistics from the GSW7 dataset (water occurrence and recurrence, 1984–2021). The model, trained on GSW’s monthly historical data, generates monthly water mask outputs. For simplicity, batch normalization and dropout layers are not shown in the figure.
